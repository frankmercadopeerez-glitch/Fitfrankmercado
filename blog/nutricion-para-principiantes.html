Nutrition for Beginners: What You Really Need to Know

Frank Mercado

February 10, 2026 • 10 min read

Forget everything you've heard about "clean eating," "superfoods," and "detoxes." Nutrition doesn't have to be complicated. In this guide, I'll break down the fundamentals that actually matter for your fitness goals.

The Only Rule That Truly Matters

Energy balance. That's it. If you want to lose weight, you need to eat fewer calories than you burn. If you want to gain muscle, you need to eat more. Everything else is just optimization.

This isn't opinion—it's physics. The law of thermodynamics applies to your body just like everything else in the universe. No food is "fattening" in isolation; it's only the total amount that matters.

Understanding Macronutrients

All food is made up of three macronutrients: protein, carbohydrates, and fats. Each serves a purpose:

Protein

The building block of muscle. When you're training, you need more protein than a sedentary person. Aim for 1.6-2.2 grams per kilogram of body weight.

Good sources: chicken, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, tofu.

Carbohydrates

Your body's preferred energy source, especially for high-intensity exercise. Carbs are not the enemy—they fuel your workouts and recovery.

Good sources: rice, oats, potatoes, fruits, bread, pasta.

Fats

Essential for hormone production, brain function, and nutrient absorption. Don't fear fats, but be mindful of portions since they're calorie-dense.

Good sources: olive oil, avocado, nuts, fatty fish, eggs.

How to Calculate Your Calories

Here's a simple formula to estimate your daily calorie needs:

  1. Multiply your weight in kg by 22-26 (lower end if sedentary, higher if very active)
  2. This gives you your maintenance calories
  3. To lose fat: subtract 300-500 calories
  4. To gain muscle: add 200-300 calories

Monitor your weight for 2-3 weeks and adjust as needed. Your body is unique, and these are just starting points.

The 80/20 Rule

Here's the secret to sustainable nutrition: aim to eat whole, nutritious foods 80% of the time. The other 20%? Enjoy your favorite foods without guilt.

This approach works because it's realistic. No one can eat "perfectly" forever. By building flexibility into your nutrition plan, you create something you can actually sustain for life.

Practical Tips for Daily Eating

  • Eat protein at every meal: It keeps you full and supports muscle recovery
  • Fill half your plate with vegetables: High volume, low calories, lots of nutrients
  • Stay hydrated: Often we confuse thirst for hunger
  • Plan ahead: Meal prep 2-3 days of food to avoid impulsive choices
  • Don't skip meals: This usually leads to overeating later
  • Eat slowly: It takes 20 minutes to feel full

What About Supplements?

Most supplements are unnecessary if you eat a balanced diet. That said, a few basics can help:

  • Protein powder: Convenient when you can't get enough from food
  • Creatine: One of the few proven performance supplements
  • Vitamin D: If you don't get much sun exposure

Save your money on fat burners, BCAAs, and most other marketed supplements. They won't provide meaningful results.

Starting Your Nutrition Journey

Don't try to change everything at once. Pick one or two habits to focus on for the next two weeks:

  1. Track your food for awareness (no changes yet)
  2. Add protein to breakfast
  3. Drink more water
  4. Include vegetables at lunch and dinner

Small, consistent changes beat dramatic overhauls every time.
